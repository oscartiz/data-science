# Deep Dive Report: The Two Sonoras

## 1. Executive Summary
This report analyzes the specific economic conditions of the state of Sonora, Mexico, leveraging localized tortilla price data. Sonora is a unique macroeconomic laboratory due to its geography: it spans the standard Mexican economic zone and the **Zona Libre de la Frontera Norte (ZLFN)**. By comparing the trajectory of tortilla prices against these two distinct minimum wage regimes, alongside the state's critical role in agriculture and its vulnerability to drought, we uncover a stark divergence in purchasing power.

## 2. Methodology and Segmentation
To understand local purchasing power, we extracted data for the four Sonoran municipalities present in the dataset and split them into two distinct economic zones:

1. **Border Zone (ZLFN)**: Nogales, San Luis Río Colorado.
   - *Wage Regime*: Subject to the Free Northern Border Zone Minimum Wage (implemented in 2019), which mandates significantly higher baseline compensation.
2. **Non-Border Zone (SMG)**: Hermosillo, Cd. Obregón.
   - *Wage Regime*: Subject to the standard General Minimum Wage (SMG).

## 3. Findings: Purchasing Power Divergence

Our comparative growth analysis (benchmarked to 2007 = 100) reveals a drastic split between the two zones starting in 2019.

### 3.1 Non-Border Sonora (The Standard Struggle)
In municipalities like Hermosillo and Cd. Obregón, the economic narrative mirrors the national average. Tortilla prices outpaced standard minimum wage growth for over a decade. While recent SMG hikes have begun to restore purchasing power (with wages growing to nearly 492% of their 2007 value), the pressure of food inflation remains a significant hurdle.

### 3.2 Border Sonora (The Purchasing Power Boom)
In Nogales and San Luis Río Colorado, the implementation of the ZLFN policy in 2019 created an unprecedented spike in the wage growth index. The minimum wage in this region skyrocketed, vastly outpacing local tortilla price inflation. This has resulted in a massive net gain in purchasing power for minimum-wage earners along the border regarding this essential staple.

## 4. Macroeconomic Factors: Agriculture and Drought
The context of tortilla prices in Sonora cannot be fully understood without addressing the state's agricultural profile.

- **White Corn Production**: Sonora is historically one of the most critical producers of white corn (the primary ingredient in tortillas) and wheat in Mexico. The massive irrigation districts in the Yaqui and Mayo valleys dictate much of the regional supply.
- **Drought and Water Scarcity**: Sonora is highly susceptible to severe droughts. In recent years, critically low water levels in the state's dams have forced massive reductions in planting areas. 
- **Price Impacts**: When local yields fail due to drought, corn must be imported from other states (like Sinaloa) or internationally, adding massive transportation and logistical premiums to the baseline cost of tortillas in Sonora. This local supply chain vulnerability puts continuous upward pressure on the `Price per kilogram`, threatening to erode the purchasing power gains seen even in the ZLFN.

## 5. Conclusion
The implementation of the ZLFN policy has successfully shielded border municipalities in Sonora from the worst impacts of staple food inflation. However, the continuous threat of drought and its catastrophic impact on local white corn yields means that aggressive wage policies must be paired with agricultural resilience strategies to ensure long-term food security in the state.
